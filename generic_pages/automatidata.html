Regression Analysis

Simplifying Complex Data Relationships and Predicting Taxi Fares

Introduction:

In the world of data analysis and predictive modeling, regression analysis plays a vital role in uncovering relationships between variables. In this blog post, we delve into a fascinating project undertaken by the Automatidata team for the New York City Taxi and Limousine Commission (NYC TLC). The goal was to build a multiple linear regression model to predict taxi fares using existing data collected over the course of a year. Let's explore the key insights and findings from this project, showcasing the power of Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) and regression modeling.

Part 1: Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) & Checking Model Assumptions

EDA Purpose:

EDA serves several purposes before constructing a multiple linear regression model. It helps us understand the data, detect correlations, identify outliers, missing data, and duplicates. By conducting thorough EDA, we gain crucial insights into the dataset's characteristics and make informed decisions about feature selection.

Key EDA Steps:
  • Box plots were utilized to visualize and identify outliers in significant columns such as trip_distance, fare_amount, and duration.
  • Outliers were handled by imputing lower values below $0 to 0 and capping maximum values using the 75th percentile (Q3) plus six times the interquartile range (IQR).
  • Feature engineering techniques were applied to create additional variables, including mean_distance, mean_duration, pickup_dropoff, day, month, and rush_hour.

Part 2: Model Building and Evaluation

Resources Utilized:

During the model building and evaluation stage, several resources and techniques were employed to ensure accurate predictions:

  • StandardScaler from sklearn.preprocessing was used to normalize the data.
  • The dataset was split into training and testing sets, with the test set accounting for 20% of the samples.
  • LinearRegression from sklearn.linear_model was instantiated and fit to the training data.
Model Evaluation:

The multiple linear regression model yielded impressive results, as indicated by the following metrics:

  • Coefficient of determination (R^2): 0.8398
  • Mean Absolute Error (MAE): 2.187
  • Mean Squared Error (MSE): 17.890
  • Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE): 4.230

Part 3: Interpreting Model Results

Key Insights:
  • Mean_distance was identified as the feature with the greatest impact on predicting fare amounts, with an average increase of $7 per mile traveled.
  • The scatterplot of actual versus predicted values visually demonstrated the model's accuracy and ability to capture fare patterns.
  • The histogram of residuals showcased a normal distribution around zero, indicating that the model's errors were evenly distributed and unbiased.
  • The correlation matrix and heatmap revealed a strong correlation between mean_duration, mean_distance, and the target variable, fare_amount.

Business Recommendations:
  • Consider adjusting fare rates based on distance traveled, with a focus on optimizing revenue by aligning with passenger preferences.
  • Leverage the mean_duration variable to estimate trip durations accurately and provide customers with more reliable time estimates.
  • Explore the impact of rush_hour trips on fare amounts, potentially offering differentiated pricing or incentives during peak hours.

Conclusion:

The project successfully demonstrated the power of EDA and multiple linear regression in predicting taxi fares based on historical data. By understanding the data, creating meaningful features, and building an accurate model, valuable insights were obtained for the NYC TLC. This information can drive data-driven decision-making, optimize fare pricing strategies, and enhance the overall taxi experience. Regression analysis continues to be an essential tool in simplifying complex data relationships and unlocking predictive capabilities across various industries.
